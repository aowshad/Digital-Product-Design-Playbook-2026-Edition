Chapter 01

Foundations in the
AI Era

Design has always been about solving human problems. AI doesn't change that mandate — it radically changes the tools, the medium, and the expectations on both sides of the screen.

Then & Now
Shifts
Principles
Role

The Three Shifts Every Designer Must Understand

Between 2022 and 2026, generative AI rewired the relationship between designers, their tools, and their users. Three fundamental shifts define this new reality. None of them are about the latest model release — they're about how the medium itself has changed.

01
From screens to conversations
Interfaces increasingly respond in natural language. Designing a chat or voice interaction requires entirely different skills than designing a CRUD screen.
02
From deterministic to probabilistic
AI outputs vary. You're no longer designing fixed states — you're designing for a range of possible outputs, including failures and surprises.
03
From features to capabilities
Users now ask "what can it do?" rather than "how do I use it?" Discoverability and expectation-setting become core design problems.
Fig. 1 · The shift in interface paradigm
Core Principle — from "The Design of Everyday Things"

"Good design is actually a lot harder to notice than poor design, in part because good designs fit our needs so well that the design is invisible." — Don Norman. AI magnifies this: when it works perfectly, users feel smart. When it fails, they feel betrayed.

What Has Not Changed

Before chasing every new AI capability, anchor yourself in what remains constant. The principles below survived the shift from print to digital, from desktop to mobile, and from native to web. They will survive AI too.

1
Empathy is still the superpower

No model can replace a designer who genuinely understands what a person is trying to accomplish, what they fear, and what would delight them. AI accelerates your output. Empathy determines whether that output is worth anything.

2
Context is everything

From Jakob Nielsen's usability heuristics to Christopher Alexander's pattern language — context always wins. An AI feature that's brilliant for one user segment might be confusing for another. Always design for your actual users, not the median user.

3
Cognitive load is finite

Per John Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory (adapted for UX by many — including George Miller's work on working memory limits), human attention is scarce. AI-rich interfaces often increase complexity. Your job is still to reduce friction, not add wonder for wonder's sake.

4
Trust is built over many micro-moments

Patrick Lencioni's work on organizational trust and Fogg's Persuasive Technology framework both point to the same truth: trust is not given — it's accumulated. Each interaction either deposits or withdraws from the user's trust account.

Real-Life Scenario Notion AI — When capability outpaces clarity

When Notion added AI writing features in 2023, early versions surfaced AI suggestions that interrupted writing flow. Users felt the AI was "taking over." The design team iterated to make AI assistance explicitly opt-in — a small design choice with massive trust implications.

↳ Lesson

Showing users where AI ends and their content begins isn't just an ethical concern — it's a fundamental UX problem. Control and transparency are features.

The Designer's Role in AI Products

A useful mental model: think of yourself as the translator between human intent and machine capability. Engineers build what's possible. Product managers define what's valuable. Designers determine what's usable, trustworthy, and humane.

From "Designing with the Mind in Mind" — Jeff Johnson

The brain perceives patterns before it processes details. In AI interfaces, users form a "mental model" of the AI in the first 30 seconds. Design the first interaction to build the right model — not just to showcase capability.

Key Reference Works

Basic Books, Revised Edition 2013
Affordances, signifiers, and feedback loops remain the grammar of all interaction design — AI or not.

Research &
Discovery

Research is the act of manufacturing clarity out of uncertainty. AI gives you unprecedented speed — but speed without rigor produces confident nonsense. Know when to use which tool.

The Research Landscape

Research methods fall on two axes: quantitative vs. qualitative, and attitudinal vs. behavioral. The best researchers triangulate across all four quadrants. AI currently excels in the quantitative-attitudinal zone and struggles with nuanced behavioral observation.

Fig. 2 · Research methods across four quadrants
MethodTypeAI-augmentable?Best for
User interviewsQualitative / AttitudinalPartialMotivations, mental models, emotional context
Usability testingQualitative / BehavioralPartialFinding where users struggle in real tasks
SurveysQuantitative / AttitudinalYesMeasuring attitudes at scale
Analytics / HeatmapsQuantitative / BehavioralYesWhat users actually do vs. what they say
Diary studiesQualitative / BehavioralPartialLongitudinal behavior in real contexts
Contextual inquiryQualitative / BehavioralNoDeep situational understanding

AI-Augmented Research Workflow

Modern research is no longer a sprint of solo synthesis. It's a series of AI-assisted micro-steps, each requiring human judgment at the inflection points where context matters most.

01
Screener & Recruitment Brief

Use AI to draft screener surveys and recruitment criteria from your research goals. Prompt: "Write a screener to recruit users who have used AI-assisted writing tools at least 3 times but are not power users."

02
Interview Guide Generation

AI can draft a discussion guide, but you must edit it. The best questions are born from a specific hypothesis — AI doesn't have your context or project history.

03
Transcription & First-Pass Synthesis

Tools like Dovetail, Grain, or Otter.ai transcribe and auto-tag themes. Treat AI-identified themes as hypotheses, not conclusions. Always read raw transcripts.

04
Affinity Mapping (AI-assisted)

FigJam's AI clustering can group hundreds of sticky notes in seconds. The human job is curation — naming clusters, merging redundant themes, spotting the insight hiding between clusters.

05
Insight → Opportunity Translation

Insights describe what is true. Opportunities describe what could be better. This translation requires human judgment — AI can suggest framing but cannot decide what matters.

Sample AI Prompt — Interview Synthesis
Here are 6 interview transcripts from users of our expense tracking app. Each user was asked about their biggest pain points with expense reporting. Task: 1. Identify the top 3 recurring themes across all interviews 2. For each theme, provide 2 direct quotes that best represent it 3. Note any contradictions or outlier perspectives 4. Do NOT suggest solutions — only surface patterns Transcripts: [paste here]
Pro tip: Ask for contradictions explicitly. AI models are trained to find consensus; outliers often hold the most interesting design opportunities.
Framework — from "Interviewing Users" by Steve Portigal

"The best interview questions are deceptively simple. 'Tell me about the last time you...' opens doors that 'Do you ever...' closes." Behavioral questions about past actions predict future behavior better than hypothetical questions.

Good vs. weak research questions

Weak QuestionStronger AlternativeWhy Better
Do users like dark mode?In what contexts do users switch display modes, and what triggers that decision?Behavioral context > binary preference
Is our onboarding good?Where do users drop off during onboarding and what did they expect instead?Specific, measurable, actionable

Real-Life Scenario Spotify Wrapped — Research-led personalization

Spotify's annual Wrapped feature began as an internal data experiment. User researchers noticed that users talked about "their music personality" in interviews — an attitudinal insight. Behavioral data showed users screenshotting their listening stats. The combination revealed an unmet need: music as identity expression.

↳ Design Lesson

The most valuable research insight often lives at the intersection of what users say (attitudinal) and what they do (behavioral). Neither alone would have revealed the Wrapped opportunity.

Chapter 03

Problem
Framing

The quality of your solution is bounded by the quality of your problem statement. A poorly framed problem guarantees a well-executed solution to the wrong thing.

The How Might We Framework (Evolved)

The classic IDEO "How Might We" question remains one of the most powerful problem-framing tools. In the AI era, it requires an additional layer: distinguishing problems that AI can meaningfully address from those that require human design solutions.

narrow
HMW make the loading spinner faster?
Focuses on a symptom, not the underlying problem. Limits creative space.
broad
HMW make our app more delightful?
Too vague to inspire focused ideation. Could mean anything.
right
HMW help anxious first-time investors make a first trade without feeling overwhelmed?
Names the user, context, emotion, and behavior. Specific enough to generate solutions.

Jobs-to-be-Done in Practice

Clayton Christensen's Jobs-to-be-Done framework, popularized for product design by Alan Klement's "When Coffee and Kale Compete," reframes design from feature-building to job-solving. Users "hire" products to do a job. When a better solution exists, they "fire" your product.

Core Framework — JTBD Job Statement

"When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]." This structure forces you to capture context, not just need. Example: "When I'm waiting for a meeting to start, I want to quickly catch up on unread messages, so I can walk in with relevant context."

Three levels of jobs

→
Functional Job
The practical task: "Send money to a friend." This is the baseline — most products address this and compete on price or convenience.
◎
Emotional Job
How the user wants to feel: "Feel confident I sent the right amount." This is where most design decisions are actually made.
◈
Social Job
How the user wants to be perceived: "Look generous and easy to deal with." Venmo's social feed is built entirely on the social job.
Fig. 3 · The job layered three ways
Real-Life Scenario Airbnb — Reframing the problem

In 2012, Airbnb was struggling. The team's initial problem frame: "How might we increase bookings?" This led to tactical solutions — better search, lower prices. Contextual research revealed the real issue: hosts were using low-quality cameras to photograph their spaces. The actual problem was trust through presentation.

↳ Reframe

They hired professional photographers for $80 and sent them to host homes. Bookings doubled in New York within a week. The solution was a human photographer — not a product feature. Correct problem framing led to a non-obvious solution.

The Problem Statement Canvas

Before writing a single design brief, fill in this structure. Skip a field and you're designing blind. Tick each item as you complete it — the count tracks your progress.

Problem statement canvas 0 / 6 complete
User · Who specifically are we designing for? (Segment, context, expertise level)
Avoid "all users." Narrower users → sharper design.
Situation · In what context does this problem arise? When, where, under what conditions?
Time and place matter enormously to design decisions.
Obstacle · What specific friction or pain is happening? (Evidence-based, not assumed)
Use direct quotes from research when possible.
Impact · What is the consequence if this problem persists?
Quantify where possible — drop-off rate, support tickets, NPS verbatims.
Current workarounds · How are users solving this today, however clumsily?
Workarounds reveal unmet needs and hint at solutions.
Success definition · How would we know we've solved this well?
Set this before ideating — otherwise you'll fall in love with clever solutions to wrong problems.

Chapter 04

Ideation &
Concepts

Ideas are cheap. The ability to generate them quickly and select them wisely is the design skill AI has most disrupted — and most amplified.

AI as an Ideation Partner

Generative AI is an excellent brainstorming partner — it produces volume without fatigue. But volume is not the goal. The goal is the unexplored corner of the solution space that changes how you see the problem. Use AI to stress-test your own thinking, not to replace it.

Sample AI Prompt — Divergent Ideation
Problem: New users of our budgeting app abandon the onboarding flow at the "Connect your bank account" step. Generate 20 concepts — not features — that could address this. Include: - 5 ideas that remove the step entirely - 5 ideas that reframe what connecting a bank account means - 5 ideas that address the underlying fear (privacy, security, complexity) - 5 ideas that are obviously bad but might contain a hidden insight Rate each 1–5 on novelty (not feasibility). Focus on the high-novelty ones.
Requesting "obviously bad ideas" unlocks lateral thinking. Bad ideas often contain the seed of a breakthrough reframe.

Concept Selection: The 2×2 Matrix

After ideation, the hardest design skill is selection. This matrix from "Sprint" (Jake Knapp, Google Ventures) separates exploration from exploitation:

↑↓
Quick Wins · Do First
High impact, low effort. Often existing patterns applied to new contexts. Ship and measure.
↑↑
Strategic Bets · Prototype
High impact, high effort. Requires a design sprint to derisk before committing.
↓↓
Fill-ins · Maybe Later
Low impact, low effort. Consider only if they don't conflict with higher priorities.
↓↑
Thankless · Avoid
Low impact, high effort. Ruthlessly deprioritize unless business constraints force otherwise.
From "Shape Up" — Ryan Singer (Basecamp)

"Shaped work includes a problem definition, an approach, and a constraint — but leaves room for the team to work out the details. Appetite (time budget) shapes everything. A 6-week problem is designed differently than a 2-week one."

Real-Life Scenario Linear — Opinionated design as strategy

Linear became a cult product among engineering teams not by generating the most ideas, but by ruthlessly saying no. Every feature concept is evaluated against a single design principle: "Does this make fast teams faster?" Features that don't serve elite users are removed even if they'd serve more users.

↳ Design Lesson

Before ideating, define your design thesis. Linear's is "speed." Figma's is "collaboration." Ideation is not about volume — it's about finding the concept that serves your thesis.

Chapter 05

Designing
AI Interactions

AI interfaces introduce a category of design problems no previous generation of designers has faced: how to design for an output you cannot fully predict.

The Five Core Challenges of AI UX

Every AI feature inherits these six challenges by default. The best AI products don't avoid them — they address each one explicitly through design.

01
Expectation Gap
Users either over-trust or under-trust AI. Neither is safe. Design to calibrate expectations accurately — not optimistically.
02
Output Variability
The same prompt can produce different outputs. Design needs to handle the full distribution, including hallucinations and refusals.
03
Latency
AI takes time. Users who waited 300ms for page loads now face 10-second response times. Streaming UIs and progressive disclosure are essential.
04
Input Design
Natural language is infinite. Designing for open-ended prompts is fundamentally different from form design. Empty states and examples become critical.
05
Error States
AI errors are not 404s — they're contextual, nuanced, and often not recognizable as errors. Design for graceful degradation and recovery.
06
Attribution & Trust
When AI generates content for real people, how does the interface communicate provenance, confidence, and fallibility?
Fig. 4 · The trust calibration curve

Design Patterns for AI Interfaces

Pattern 1 — Suggested starting points

An empty chat input is one of the most intimidating UIs ever designed. Show 3–4 example prompts that match common user jobs. Make them specific enough to set accurate expectations. This pattern, used by ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, and Gemini, dramatically reduces bounce rate on first use.

Pattern 2 — Progressive disclosure of confidence

When the AI is less certain, show it. This could be as simple as "I'm not sure about this — you might want to verify" or as complex as source citations (like Perplexity). Users who understand AI limitations are better users.

Pattern 3 — Streaming output

Showing text as it's generated dramatically reduces perceived latency. It also helps users understand what the model is doing — and allows them to interrupt when the direction is wrong, saving time for both user and system.

Pattern 4 — Undo, edit, regenerate

Every AI output should have a lightweight path to modification. These three controls cover 80% of user recovery needs from suboptimal outputs.

From Google's "People + AI Guidebook" (PAIR)

"Design for when AI gets it wrong. If you only design for success, you've designed half a product. AI errors create disproportionate trust damage — a single bad output can undo dozens of good ones."

Designing for Trust Calibration

The goal is not maximum trust — it's accurate trust. A user who trusts an AI too much will rely on it in situations where it's likely to fail.

Trust LevelUser BehaviorDesign Response
OvertrustAccepts all output without verification. Delegates high-stakes decisions.Add friction: "Review before submitting." Confidence indicators. Citation links.
Calibrated TrustUses AI appropriately. Verifies when stakes are high. Understands limitations.Reinforce with transparency. Show why AI suggested X. Enable easy correction.
UndertrustIgnores AI suggestions. Double-checks everything. Feels AI is unreliable.Demonstrate capability gradually. Show value in low-stakes tasks first.
Real-Life Scenario GitHub Copilot — Trust through transparency

GitHub Copilot's initial UX showed AI suggestions inline as "ghost text." Engineers accepted suggestions without always understanding them. Studies found both over-acceptance (wrong code) and under-acceptance (dismissing correct suggestions). The team introduced "Copilot Chat" — allowing engineers to ask why a suggestion was made. This transparency layer improved both trust calibration and productivity.

↳ Design Lesson

The interface for an AI suggestion and the interface for understanding that suggestion are two different design problems. Both matter. Explainability is not a bonus — it's a trust mechanism.

Prototyping
& Testing

A prototype is a question made tangible. The best prototypes answer the riskiest questions with the least effort. AI has fundamentally changed what's possible to prototype in an afternoon.

Fidelity ≠ Quality

Match your fidelity to your question. A high-fidelity prototype that answers the wrong question wastes everyone's time. A paper sketch that answers the right question in 20 minutes is brilliant design.

QuestionRight FidelityWhy
"Are we solving the right problem?"Storyboard or concept docNo interface needed to validate a job
"Will users understand this flow?"Clickable wireframes (mid-fi)Navigation clarity doesn't require polish
"Do users trust this UI?"High-fidelityTrust signals are in the details
"Is this copy clear?"Text prototypeContent clarity doesn't need visuals
"Will this AI behavior feel right?"Wizard of OzReveals UX issues cheaply before model code

The Wizard of Oz Technique for AI Features

Before spending engineering cycles on an AI feature, test the experience manually. A human "wizard" simulates the AI in real-time while the user interacts normally.

01
Define the AI's role

What exactly will the AI do? Write it in one sentence. "The AI will read the user's uploaded resume and suggest 5 improvements." Vagueness in the prototype creates confusion in the test.

02
Build the shell UI

Create the interface that will show AI output. Leave the output area blank — the wizard fills it in real-time. A Figma frame shared via Figma Mirror works fine.

03
Run the session

While the participant interacts, the wizard types simulated responses from a separate device. Introduce intentional errors — test how the user handles suboptimal output.

04
Debrief on the gap

Ask: "Did the AI output match what you expected? What would have made it more useful?" The gap between expected and received is your design brief for the AI output layer.

From "Sprint" — Jake Knapp

"You can prototype almost anything in a day. You don't need it to work perfectly — you need it to appear to work. The goal is to get the right data, not to build the right product. That comes after."

Usability Testing Checklist for AI Products

AI features fail differently than traditional UI. These four checks will surface problems most usability tests miss.

AI testing checklist 0 / 4 complete
Run each task scenario with at least 3 different AI responses (good, mediocre, incorrect)
Observe how users respond to each — this reveals error recovery design needs.
Measure task completion rate AND prompt revision rate
A user who revises their prompt 5 times to get a good answer has a usability problem even if they eventually succeed.
Ask users to think aloud about what they expected vs. what they received
The expectation gap is your most important AI UX signal.
Observe trust calibration: do users verify AI output? When? How?
Note when users blindly accept incorrect outputs — a critical safety and UX failure.

Craft &
Visual Systems

In an era where AI can generate UI in seconds, the value of taste, intentionality, and system-thinking has never been higher. Speed without craft produces volume without value.

Design Systems Are Infrastructure

A design system is not a component library. It's the codified values, decisions, and patterns that make a product coherent at scale. The best design systems — IBM Carbon, Material Design, Apple HIG, Atlassian Design System — work because they encode decisions, not just designs.

L1
Design Tokens
The atoms: color, typography, spacing, border radius, shadow. Use semantic naming (color-action-primary, not blue-500).
L2
Components
Reusable UI units. A component should do one thing well. Composability over omniscience.
L3
Patterns
Recurring interaction solutions: search, pagination, forms, notifications. Patterns compose components with interaction logic.
L4
Templates
Page-level layouts. Where navigation, spacing, and density decisions live at the macro level.
Fig. 5 · Design system hierarchy

Typography: The Most Underrated Design Tool

Robert Bringhurst's "The Elements of Typographic Style" argues that "typography exists to honor content." In product design, typography is how your interface whispers or shouts, calms or energizes, includes or excludes.

Color with Intent

The most common color mistake in product design is treating color as decoration. Color is communication. Every color decision should answer: what does this color tell the user about this element's role, state, or priority?

Framework
The 60-30-10 Rule — Updated for AI Interfaces
60% Neutral: Background, surfaces, containers. In AI products, this includes the AI output area — it should be calm, high-contrast, and scannable.

30% Brand/Primary: Navigation, key actions, interactive elements. Personality lives here — but it must serve clarity.

10% Accent/Semantic: Errors, success states, warnings, AI-generated content markers. Use sparingly. When everything is highlighted, nothing is.

Accessibility Is Not Optional

Approximately 1 in 5 people has a disability that affects how they use digital products. Designing accessibly is both ethically necessary and commercially smart. Tick each item as you verify your design meets it.

Accessibility checklist 0 / 5 complete
Text contrast minimum 4.5:1 for body, 3:1 for large text (WCAG 2.1 AA)
Use Figma's built-in contrast checker or the A11y Color Contrast plugin.
All interactive elements keyboard-accessible
Tab order, focus states, and keyboard shortcuts should be designed — not left to browser defaults.
AI-generated content is clearly labeled for screen readers
Use ARIA roles and labels. Visually-distinct AI content must also be semantically distinct.
Loading states use both animation and text for status
Never rely on a spinner alone. "Generating your response..." serves users who can't see animation.
Color is not the only means of conveying information
Add iconography, pattern, or text alongside color-coded status indicators.
From "Inclusive Design Patterns" — Heydon Pickering

"Accessibility isn't about edge cases — it's about designing for the full range of human variability. When you design for extremes, you create a better experience for everyone."

Motion Design in AI Products

AI introduces motion states that didn't exist before. Each needs its own vocabulary — borrowing from traditional UI motion will feel either too aggressive or too sleepy.

StateMotion ApproachExample
Processing / ThinkingLooping soft pulse or shimmer (0.8–1.2s loop)A subtle waveform or breathing indicator — not a spinning circle.
Generating / StreamingCascade or typewriter — organic, not mechanicalText appearing word-by-word. Fast enough to feel alive, slow enough to read.
Error / RefusalGentle shake or dissolve — never aggressiveInput field softly shakes. Content fades to neutral state.
Success / CompleteBrief micro-celebration (200–300ms scale or flash)A checkmark that scales up and settles. Not a slot machine win.

Measuring
Design Impact

Design that can't be measured can't be defended. In AI-era products, the metrics that matter have expanded — and some traditional conversion metrics have become dangerously misleading.

The North Star Problem

A north star metric answers: "If this single number improves, is our product genuinely getting better for users?" Most companies track north stars that are proxies for value, not value itself. Optimizing proxies produces Goodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

From "Continuous Discovery Habits" — Teresa Torres

"Outcome-based roadmaps focus on the change in human behavior that drives business results. Feature-based roadmaps focus on output. The best design teams are measured on outcomes, not output."

The HEART Framework (Google Ventures)

Developed by Kerry Rodden at Google, the HEART framework maps user-centered metrics to product goals. It remains one of the most rigorous frameworks for measuring design quality at scale.

H
Happiness
User satisfaction, NPS, CSAT. Attitudinal. Measured via surveys. Caution: users can be happy with bad products.
E
Engagement
Depth of interaction: sessions, actions per session, content created. Not just logins — meaningful engagement with core value.
A
Adoption
New users or features activated. Tracks growth and discoverability. Important for AI features users don't know exist.
R
Retention
Users returning over time. The most honest signal of product value. Hard to game.
T
Task Success
Completion rate, error rate, time-on-task. In AI products: add "prompt revision rate" as a hidden usability signal.

AI-Specific Metrics

Traditional dashboards miss the most important AI signals. These five metrics belong in every AI product's analytics — and most of them are absent from the standard toolkit.

MetricWhat it measuresWhy it matters
Output acceptance rate% of AI suggestions accepted without modificationHigh acceptance + high task success = well-calibrated AI. High acceptance + low success = overtrust.
Prompt revision rateAvg. reprompts per successful taskMore than 2–3 reprompts signals input design or expectation-setting failure.
Trust recovery rate% of users who return after an AI errorMeasures resilience of the user-AI relationship. Good error design improves this.
Output verification rate% of AI outputs users independently verifyLow in high-stakes contexts = overtrust warning. High in low-stakes = friction problem.
Feature discovery rate% of users who discover AI capabilities organicallyLow rate = poor empty states, onboarding, or in-product guidance design.
Real-Life Scenario Duolingo — North star over vanity metrics

Duolingo rejected DAU (daily active users) as its north star. Heavy reliance on streaks drove DAU but also drove anxiety-based engagement — users who hated the app but kept opening it to protect their streak. Their redesigned north star: "Days Active Learning" — where learning meant completing a meaningful lesson, not just opening the app. This nuanced metric change led to product decisions that reduced streak anxiety while improving actual learning outcomes.

↳ Design Lesson

In AI products, beware of optimizing for AI usage (sessions, queries) over AI value (tasks completed, user outcomes achieved). Heavy AI usage with low user value is a symptom, not a goal.

Final Reflection: The Designer's Responsibility

AI has made many things faster and cheaper to build. It has not made it faster or cheaper to understand what deserves to be built — or what the consequences of building it might be. That judgment remains the designer's fundamental responsibility.

Closing Thought

"Design is not the veneer on top of a product. Design is the product's relationship with the people who use it. In an age of AI, designers are the guardians of that relationship — the translators between capability and humanity, between what machines can do and what people actually need."
